As city doubles down on B-HEARD mental health crisis response, some advocates say it is not enough

By Amira McKee and Tomas Dias Piva Imparato

Graphics by Mehr Kaur

October 17, 2022

Photo by Gabriella Gregor-Splaver

A year ago, New York City piloted the Behavioral Health Emergency Assistance Response Division in West Harlem. Since then, B-HEARD, which the city has advertised as a nonpolice response to mental health crises, has become the center of a citywide debate on policing and crisis response. While some advocates laud the program for its departure from traditional police-led programs, others question its efficacy.

This month, the program will expand to service four additional precincts in the Bronx and Brooklyn, reigniting controversy over its effectiveness as a solution to police violence against New Yorkers with mental illness. Though Mayor Eric Adams’ administration has touted the program as a success, allocating an additional $55.3 million to the project in its 2023 fiscal budget, some activists are concerned by the project’s limited scope. In Harlem and the Bronx, only 16 percent of mental health-related 911 calls were answered by B-HEARD teams between January and March.

Former Mayor Bill de Blasio announced the first iteration of the program in 2020, after a series of prominent police killings of New Yorkers with mental health conditions in recent years. People with untreated mental health conditions are especially vulnerable to police violence, according to the Treatment Advocacy Center, and are 16 times more likely to be killed while interacting with law enforcement.

“Experiencing a mental health crisis is not a crime,” Pascale Leone, executive director of the Supportive Housing Network of New York, said in a press release provided to Spectator. “It certainly should not—and must not—be a death sentence.”

B-HEARD teams are staffed by EMTs and paramedics from the Fire Department’s Emergency Medical Services and mental health professionals from NYC Health + Hospitals. Modeled off of similar programs in cities such as Eugene, Oregon, and Miami, Florida, teams of trained healthcare workers began responding to New Yorkers’ mental health-related 911 calls in spring 2021. However, the program did not begin operating under the B-HEARD name in East and Central Harlem until June of that year, and only expanded to West Harlem in November.

Since B-HEARD’s launch, EMT and mental health professionals have accompanied or replaced police in only a limited number of crises. In June 2021, its first month in operation, B-HEARD teams responded to 20 percent of 911 mental health crisis calls in their coverage areas. From January to March this year, however, response rates fell by 4 percentage points. While 383 mental health crisis calls were addressed by B-HEARD teams, the remaining 2,017 calls were left to the New York Police Department alone.

911 operators routed a quarter of mental health crisis calls to B-HEARD in June 2021, and the city projected that number to increase to half in the following months. Between January and March 2022, however, the number of calls routed to B-HEARD instead fell to 23 percent of all mental health crisis 911 calls. Among these calls explicitly routed to the program by 911 operators, B-HEARD responses also fell by 12 percentage points after the program expanded, to 68 percent.

The remaining calls, according to the city, were “typically” left to traditional responses because B-HEARD teams were occupied by another call.

NYPD respondents also have the option to request onsite assistance from B-HEARD, an opportunity only exercised 14 times in June 2021, the first month of the program’s operation.

Amidst criticism of the program’s scope, Adams bolstered B-HEARD funding, allowing for its continued expansion. According to the city’s data, the program has succeeded in increasing the number of people who accept assistance, compared to the traditional NYPD or EMS response. Professor Jeffrey Coots, director of John Jay College of Criminal Justice’s From Punishment to Public Health initiative, said the B-HEARD model, in its current iteration, is useful but not a sufficient solution to addressing mental health crises.

“I think [B-HEARD] is a valuable tool,” Coots said. “But it’s not a silver bullet. And we can’t pretend that because we have B-HEARD, now we don’t have problems with police response to mental health issues, or a disproportionate demand versus the capacity to respond to mental health issues in our community.”

Community Board 9 health and environment committee co-chairs, Edwin Torres and LaQuita Henry also have concerns about a lack of West Harlem community input regarding the rollout of B-HEARD in the neighborhood.

“We wish there was more conversation with us in the community board, because we get to hear from different folks,” Torres said. “It takes a village in order for us to take care of another vulnerable population that suffers from lack of health access. … So it’s not only about having an urgent team respond, but, I would say, what happens next? Because if we keep just responding to crisis after crisis, we’re not treating, we’re just being reactive.”

In commemoration of the five-year anniversary of the death of Miguel Richards, who was killed by law enforcement in the Bronx while experiencing a mental health crisis in 2017, members of the Correct Crisis Intervention Today in New York City coalition rallied for a nonpolice response to mental health crisis calls on Sept. 29. The coalition, composed of organizations working to address police violence against those with mental illness, was joined by community leaders, including District 9 Council Member Kristin Richardson Jordan, to advocate for care led by peers to replace the “current police-led” program.

“We urge city leaders to work with CCIT-NYC to adapt the B-HEARD model to include peers with lived experience as trained first responders and to stop deploying NYPD for mental health crisis calls,” Cal Hedigan, the CEO of Community Access, a housing and mental health nonprofit and a CCIT-NYC coalition member, wrote in a statement.

Ruth Lowenkron, director of disability justice at New York Lawyers for the Public Interest, a CCIT-NYC coalition member, said that while B-HEARD offers an improvement from traditional response, it remains police-led.

“The first [issue] is that the city suggests that the program is a nonpolice model, and it just simply isn’t,” Lowenkron said.

In New York City, NYPD officers respond to mental health crisis calls dispatched through 911. B-HEARD teams may accompany or replace police, or not respond at all, at the discretion of the operator. CCIT-NYC advocates for a different model, in which mental health crisis calls would be addressed by an independent program without NYPD involvement. The proposed program would partner an EMT with a de-escalator who has a history of lived mental health experience. CCIT-NYC is currently seeking $16.5 million from the city to pilot the program.

Despite its controversy, Lowenkron said she still sees B-HEARD as an important advancement from previous, police-only protocols toward safer and more sustainable alternatives.

“One thing that we feel we’ve already done very successfully is gotten the city to at least recognize the need for it,” Lowenkron said. “B-HEARD is a problematic program, but it at least attempts to minimize police, and that’s a step in the right direction.”
